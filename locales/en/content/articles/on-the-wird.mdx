*From a teaching circle, translated from Arabic.*

***

A student came to the Shaykh and said: "My days are very full. I am working, raising children, there is much pressure. Can I do the wird every other day?"

The Shaykh was quiet for a moment. Then he said:

"Do you eat every other day?"

"No, Shaykh."

"Do you sleep every other day?"

"No."

"Then why should the wird be different? The body needs food and rest every day. The heart needs its sustenance every day. If you stop feeding it, it becomes ill — slowly, without you noticing, until one day you look and find it empty."

***

## The two times

The wird of the Karkariya is prescribed for morning and evening — *bushra wa ashiyya*. This mirrors the Quranic command: "Remember your Lord much, and glorify Him morning and evening" (3:41).

Morning and evening are the hinges of the day. The wird at Fajr orients the whole waking day toward God. The wird at Maghrib seals the day and prepares the heart for the night.

## When life intervenes

The Shaykh is not harsh about the rare missed occasion due to genuine circumstance. What he addresses is the attitude of negotiating with the wird — treating it as optional, as something to fit in when convenient. That attitude is the illness, not the occasional miss.

```orbit color=orange
Q: What analogy does the Shaykh use when a student asks to do the wird every other day?
A: "Do you eat every other day? Do you sleep every other day?" — the heart needs daily sustenance just like the body.

Q: What are the two prescribed times for the wird?
A: Morning (Fajr) and evening (Maghrib) — bushra wa ashiyya, mirroring the Quranic command in 3:41.

Q: What does the Shaykh say is the real illness regarding the wird?
A: The attitude of negotiating with it — treating it as optional or something to fit in when convenient. The attitude is the illness, not the occasional miss.
```
