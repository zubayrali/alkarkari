**Faqr** (Arabic: فَقْر, "poverty") is one of the most celebrated stations of the Sufi path. It refers not to material poverty but to the spiritual state of total emptiness before God — the recognition that one possesses nothing, is nothing, and needs nothing except the divine.

## Quranic basis

> "O mankind, you are the poor (*fuqara'*) toward God, and God — He is the Rich, the Praiseworthy." (Quran 35:15)

The Sufi masters took this verse as a description of ultimate reality, not merely a metaphor.

## The Shaykh on faqr

Shaykh Mohamed Faouzi al-Karkari has spoken extensively on faqr as the very identity of the path:

> "The tariqa is faqr — and faqr is the tariqa."

In this understanding, the goal of all practice (wird, hadra, khalwa, etc.) is to arrive at this station of utter spiritual nakedness before God, which paradoxically becomes fullness.

## Faqr and fana'

Faqr is intimately related to **fana'** (annihilation) — the dissolution of the ego-self in the divine presence. Where fana' describes the *event*, faqr describes the *station*: the permanent abiding in poverty-before-God.

## The fuqara'

Members of Sufi orders are traditionally called **fuqara'** (pl. of *faqir*, "poor one") — not as an insult but as a title of honor, marking their commitment to this path.
